# How to face the recruiter (the screen)

The recruiter screen is not a formality. It is a calibration call: the recruiter decides
whether to advance you, at what level, and how to position you to the hiring manager. Ten
years in, you have real leverage — use it deliberately.

Typical format: 20-30 minutes, video or phone. One recruiter (sometimes a sourcer, sometimes
an HR business partner). No coding. Pure signal exchange.

---

## What the recruiter is actually screening for

Recruiters are not engineers. They are screening for five things:

| Signal | What they observe |
|---|---|
| Communication clarity | Can you explain your background in plain English in under two minutes? |
| Level fit | Does your scope/impact match the role (Staff vs Senior)? |
| Motivation | Why this company, why now — is this genuine? |
| Comp alignment | Will an offer land, or will you ghost after three rounds? |
| Red flags | Unexplained gaps, bad-mouthing past employers, evasiveness |

They take notes and pass a summary to the hiring manager. Your exact words travel forward.

## The 60-second pitch

Write this out and rehearse it cold. It should take 55-65 seconds at a relaxed pace.

**Template:**

> "I'm a senior engineer with [X] years of experience, the last [N] at [Company].
> My focus has been [domain: distributed systems / platform / ML infra / etc.], and the work
> I'm most proud of is [one sentence: what you built, what scale, what outcome].
> Over the last couple of years I've been operating more at the Staff level — I led
> [cross-team initiative / org-wide technical direction / etc.] that [concrete result].
> I'm looking to make that scope formal at a company where [honest, specific reason:
> the problem space, the engineering culture, the growth stage].
> That's what drew me to [Company]."

**Filled-in example (adapt freely):**

> "I'm a senior engineer with ten years of experience, the last four at Acme Corp.
> My focus has been developer platform — build systems, CI/CD, and internal tooling for
> about 600 engineers. The work I'm most proud of is a migration from a monorepo build
> graph that took 40 minutes to one that consistently runs under six, which unblocked
> three product teams from shipping faster. Over the last two years I've been the
> technical lead for the platform org's roadmap, representing us in executive reviews
> and making the call on architectural direction. I'm ready to hold that scope explicitly.
> I'm drawn to [Company] because your infrastructure problems are genuinely hard and
> I want to work somewhere that cares about internal tooling as a product."

Rules:
- No hedge words ("I sort of led...", "I was kind of responsible for...")
- One concrete metric minimum
- End with a sentence about them, not you

## The compensation-expectations question

This is the highest-stakes question in the screen. Answer it wrong and you either lowball
yourself or get screened out early. Three valid approaches:

### Option A — Give a researched range (recommended if you have data)

Research before the call. Use [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi) to look up Staff-level
total compensation at the specific company and comparable peers. Levels.fyi breaks out base,
stock (4-year total), and bonus separately — use that structure.

Sample phrasing:

> "Based on what I've seen on Levels.fyi for Staff engineers at [Company] and comparable
> companies in [city / remote], I'm targeting total compensation in the range of
> $[X]–$[Y]. That's across base, equity, and bonus on a four-year view. I'm open to
> discussing the mix depending on the equity structure here."

Anchor at or slightly above the midpoint of the range you'd actually accept. Do not give
a single number — a range gives you room to negotiate.

### Option B — Anchor without revealing your floor

If you are underpaid relative to market (common after staying at one company), avoid
anchoring to your current comp:

> "I'd rather focus on what's competitive for the role. From my research on Levels.fyi
> and conversations in the market, Staff-level engineers in this domain are earning
> $[X]–$[Y] total comp. That's the range I'm benchmarking to, not my current number."

This is legally protected language in many US states (California, New York, Colorado ban
employers from asking for current comp). Know your state.

### Option C — Defer (use sparingly, early in process)

> "I want to understand the full scope of the role before anchoring to a number.
> Can you share the band for the level you're hiring at? I'll tell you straight away
> whether it's in range."

This only works if the recruiter will actually share the band. In 2026, many companies
post bands in job listings (required by law in CA, CO, NY, WA). Check before the call —
if the band is posted, reference it directly.

**Never say:** "Whatever is fair" or "I'm flexible." It signals you have no self-knowledge
and hands all leverage to them.

## The level conversation

For Staff, this conversation matters more than at any other transition. Recruiters often
default to "we'll figure out level after the loop." Push back gently:

> "I want to make sure we're aligned on level before I invest time in the process.
> Can you tell me how the hiring committee distinguishes Staff from Senior L6 here?
> I want to calibrate my prep and make sure I'm interviewing for the right bar."

Questions to ask the recruiter about level:

- "Is this role leveled as Staff (L6/E5 equivalent) or is there flexibility if I come in
  stronger than expected?"
- "Does this team have Staff engineers already? Am I the first, or adding to an existing
  bench?"
- "What does the hiring manager say when describing the gap they're hiring for?"

The answers tell you whether they understand the Staff role themselves. If the recruiter
cannot distinguish Staff scope from Senior scope, ask to speak with the hiring manager or
an engineer on the team earlier in the process.

## Smart questions to ask (pick 2-3)

These signal strategic thinking and help you disqualify bad fits early.

| Question | Why it's good |
|---|---|
| "What problem is this team trying to solve in the next 12 months that a Staff engineer would own?" | Forces specificity; tells you if the role is real or a backfill |
| "How does the engineering org define Staff scope here — is it team-level or org-level?" | Surfaces level inflation or deflation fast |
| "What does success look like for this person at the 6-month mark?" | Shows you think in outcomes; reveals whether the role is defined |
| "What's caused Staff+ engineers to leave or move internally in the last two years?" | Politely asks about retention without sounding paranoid |
| "How are technical decisions made — is there an architecture review process, an RFC culture, something else?" | Tells you how much influence a Staff engineer actually has |
| "Is this a new headcount or a backfill?" | Backfills often have complicated dynamics worth knowing |

Avoid: "What's the culture like?" (too vague), "What are the hours?" (too early), and
anything that can be answered by the company's public careers page.

## Red flags to watch for (in the recruiter)

If you observe these, treat them as data — not disqualifiers, but worth tracking:

- Recruiter cannot describe the team's technical domain in even broad strokes
- "The level will be decided after the loop" combined with no band disclosure
- Pressure to move fast ("we have other candidates") without a credible timeline
- Vague answers about why the role is open ("growth" for a 2-year-old role that's been
  posted three times)
- No answer when you ask who you'd report to or how many people are on the team
- The role description changed materially from when you were sourced

None of these are automatic no's. They are reasons to ask sharper follow-up questions.

## Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Research comp on Levels.fyi before the call | Give your current salary as an anchor |
| Name specific impact metrics in your pitch | List responsibilities without outcomes |
| Ask about level clarity early | Assume the level is locked |
| Give a comp range with a rationale | Say "whatever is fair" |
| Take notes during the call | Multi-task or take the call in a noisy space |
| Ask about next steps and timeline at the end | Leave without knowing what happens next |
| Send a brief thank-you email within 24 hours | Disappear and wait |
| Be honest about where you are in other processes | Fabricate competing offers |

---

## Closing the call

Before you hang up, confirm:

> "What are the next steps from here, and what's a realistic timeline for the loop?
> Is there anything you need from me to keep things moving?"

This signals you are organized and interested without sounding desperate. Get the
recruiter's email so you can follow up if you go dark for more than a week.
