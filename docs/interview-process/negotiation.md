# Offer negotiation & total comp

> Audience: senior engineer (10 yrs) moving to Staff. You have leverage — use it calmly and systematically.
> Data source: [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi) for comp benchmarks and negotiation guides.
> Deep reading: [The Pragmatic Engineer](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com) — Gergely Orosz covers leveling and comp regularly.

---

## 1. Get the level right before you touch the numbers

Comp attached to the wrong level is worthless. A Staff offer at the Senior band is a pay cut disguised as a promotion.

**Why it matters**

- Level determines your equity refresh schedule, bonus target %, and promotion ceiling.
- Getting leveled down is very hard to reverse without leaving.
- Titles are inflated at many companies; ask for the internal job grade, not just the title.

**Tactics**

1. Ask the recruiter early: "Which internal level is this mapped to, and what is the typical compensation band for that level?"
2. Cross-check on [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi) — filter by company, level (L6/L7/etc.), location, and year. Sort by "Total Comp" and look at the 25th–75th percentile range.
3. If the level feels low, push before the offer letter: "Based on the scope of the role as described — owning the X system, driving cross-team technical direction — I'd expect this to be scoped at L7. Can we confirm that before discussing numbers?"
4. If the company insists on L6, ask what the L6→L7 promotion timeline looks like and get it in writing if possible.

## 2. Total comp components

Do not evaluate offers on base salary alone. Build a spreadsheet for every offer you receive.

### 2a. Component breakdown

| Component | What to ask | Watch out for |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Base salary** | Annual, paid bi-weekly or semi-monthly | Some companies cap base and compensate heavily in equity — know which model you are looking at |
| **Annual bonus** | Target % of base (e.g. 15–20% for Staff); actual payout history | Discretionary vs. formulaic; ask "what did Staff engineers actually receive the past two years?" |
| **Equity / RSUs** | Grant size, vesting schedule, refresh policy | 4-year cliff vs. monthly; whether refreshes are automatic or performance-gated |
| **Sign-on bonus** | One-time; sometimes split year 1 and year 2 | Clawback period — typically 1–2 years; if you leave early you owe it back |
| **Benefits & perks** | Health, 401k match, ESPP, parental leave | Hard to quantify but real money; a 6% 401k match on a $250k base is $15k/yr |

### 2b. Equity deep dive

RSUs are the largest variable, so model them carefully.

- **Vesting schedule**: Standard is 25% after year 1 (cliff), then monthly or quarterly over years 2–4. Some companies (Meta, Google) do back-weighted schedules — more vests later.
- **Refreshers**: Most FAANG/high-growth companies grant annual refreshers that start in year 2 or 3. Ask: "What is the typical annual refresh for a Staff engineer who is performing well?" Refreshers are often the same size as 1/4 of the initial grant.
- **Private vs. public**: Private company equity requires assumptions about liquidation event, timing, and preference stack. Apply a heavy discount (50–80%) relative to public stock. Ask for the 409A valuation and the preferred price; the ratio tells you how underwater common stock could be.
- **ESPP**: If offered, Employee Stock Purchase Plan at a 15% discount with a lookback is roughly a 15–17% guaranteed return on the portion of salary you contribute. Take the maximum if cash flow permits.

## 3. Evaluating and comparing offers: 4-year model

Build a simple spreadsheet. Never compare year-1 numbers only.

**4-year total comp template**

```
Year 1: Base + (Base × Bonus%) + RSU_year1 + Sign-on
Year 2: Base + (Base × Bonus%) + RSU_year2 + Refresh_grant_year2_portion
Year 3: Base + (Base × Bonus%) + RSU_year3 + Refresh_year2 + Refresh_year3
Year 4: Base + (Base × Bonus%) + RSU_year4 + Refresh_year2 + Refresh_year3 + Refresh_year4
Total 4-year / 4 = annual average
```

**Risk adjustment**

| Scenario | Discount factor | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Large public company (FAANG, Stripe, etc.) | 0–10% | Stock is liquid; risk is real but bounded |
| Mid-stage public (post-IPO, $10B+) | 10–25% | Volatility higher; comp data on Levels.fyi sparse |
| Late-stage private | 30–60% | Depends on liquidation preference stack and timeline |
| Early startup | 70–90% | Treat equity as lottery ticket; negotiate higher base to compensate |

## 4. Anchoring and the negotiation sequence

**The sequence that works**

1. Let them make the first offer. Do not share your current comp if you can avoid it (it is illegal to require in many US states). If pressed: "I'd prefer to understand the band for this role before anchoring to a number."
2. Express genuine enthusiasm, then ask for time. "I'm really excited about this role and the team. Can I have until [date] to review the details?"
3. Research. Pull [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi) data. Talk to your network. Know the market P75.
4. Counter in writing (email is fine). Reference market data. Be specific — name numbers.
5. Negotiate each component separately; concessions on one do not close others.

**Anchoring to market data — example email**

> Hi [Recruiter name],
>
> Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team. I've done some research and, based on [Levels.fyi data for Staff engineers at comparable companies in [city/remote]], the market rate at the 75th percentile for this level is approximately $X total comp annually. With that context, I'd like to ask for:
>
> - Base: $[X]
> - Equity: $[Y] over 4 years
> - Sign-on: $[Z] (to offset unvested equity I'm leaving behind)
>
> I want to make this work and I'm committed to the role. Can we close the gap here?

Do not apologize for negotiating. This is expected.

## 5. Competing offers

A competing offer is the single most effective leverage tool. Use it transparently and honestly.

**Rules**

- Never fabricate an offer. If it comes out, the relationship is over.
- You do not have to name the competing company. You can say "a competing offer" and describe the comp range.
- Deadlines from other companies create real urgency — let the recruiter know early so they can expedite.

**Script — sharing a competing offer**

> "I want to be transparent with you: I have a competing offer at [L7 / Staff level] with a total comp of approximately $[X]. [Company you prefer] is my first choice because [genuine reason]. Is there any flexibility to move closer to that number so I can make this an easy decision?"

If they counter, great. If they say the band is fixed, ask what flexibility exists on sign-on or equity acceleration.

## 6. The "is this the best you can do?" script

Use this after your first counter is rejected or when the recruiter says "this is our best offer."

> "I understand there are constraints — I appreciate you going to bat for me. Help me understand: is there any flexibility at all, or is this truly the ceiling? I want to be straightforward with you — I'm prepared to sign this week if we can get to $[X]. What would it take to get there?"

**Why it works**: You are not accusing them of lying. You are giving them an explicit, low-friction path to yes ("sign this week"). Recruiters have incentive to close; give them a reason.

Silence is also a tool. After making your counter, stop talking. The first person to speak loses leverage.

## 7. What NOT to do

| Mistake | Why it costs you |
|---------|-----------------|
| Accepting on the spot | Leaves money on the table; no company expects immediate acceptance |
| Negotiating verbally without following up in writing | Creates ambiguity; always confirm agreed numbers by email |
| Sharing your current salary eagerly | Anchors the offer to your past, not the market |
| Making ultimatums you are not prepared to act on | Destroys credibility; only threaten to walk if you will actually walk |
| Negotiating only base | Equity and sign-on often have more room than base |
| Apologizing for negotiating | Signals inexperience; negotiation is professional and expected |
| Ghosting or going dark | Ruins the relationship; even if you decline, do it gracefully |

## 8. Realistic Staff ranges (2026)

These are rough reference ranges only. Variance is extremely high by company tier, location, and negotiation skill. Always verify on [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi) for your specific target.

| Company tier | Geography | Base | Total comp (cash + equity annualized) |
|--------------|-----------|------|---------------------------------------|
| FAANG / top-tier (Google, Meta, Apple, Amazon) | Bay Area / NYC | $250k–$330k | $450k–$900k+ |
| FAANG | Remote / other US | $220k–$290k | $380k–$700k |
| Strong tech (Stripe, Airbnb, Databricks, etc.) | Bay Area | $230k–$310k | $350k–$650k |
| Strong tech | Remote / other US | $200k–$270k | $280k–$500k |
| Mid-market tech / Series D+ startup | Any US | $190k–$250k | $220k–$400k |
| Enterprise / non-tech org | Any US | $170k–$230k | $200k–$310k |

> Note: "Remote" ranges have been converging toward on-site since 2023. Companies are reducing remote premiums. Verify current data rather than assuming.

## 9. When a negotiation coach helps

Consider hiring a compensation negotiation coach (many operate on contingency — 10–15% of the raise they win you) if:

- The offer involves private company equity you cannot value yourself.
- Total comp is above $400k and a 5% improvement is worth $20k+.
- You have multiple competing offers at different companies and need help structuring the sequence.
- You feel emotionally unable to push back confidently — the coach negotiates on your behalf or gives you scripts and rehearsal.

Search: "compensation negotiation coach" + your target company or sector. Josh Doody (Fearless Salary Negotiation) and Hana Mohan are two practitioners with public reputations. Confirm current availability and rates.

## 10. After the offer is signed

- Get every agreed term confirmed in your offer letter before you sign.
- Keep your negotiation notes — they are useful data for your next move.
- Build your [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi) data point; it helps the community that helped you.
- Set a calendar reminder 18 months out to check your comp against market. Staff engineers who stay flat for 3+ years typically fall to P40 or lower of their own band.

---

## Quick reference: negotiation checklist

- [ ] Confirmed internal level and band before negotiating numbers
- [ ] Pulled Levels.fyi P50 and P75 for this level, company tier, and location
- [ ] Built 4-year total comp model for each offer
- [ ] Identified which component has the most room (equity or sign-on, usually)
- [ ] Sent counter in writing with specific numbers and market data reference
- [ ] Shared competing offer (if real) or indicated competing process
- [ ] Confirmed all final terms in the offer letter text before signing
